من العتبة

Min al-Ataba · From the Threshold

A reflective leadership journal in four stations. CD 531 Leadership and Transformation · MA Community Development, University of Victoria · Summer 2026 · Hosam

Concept

Four words, four scripts, one argument

This journal is built on four Arabic words, each carrying a concept that English leadership vocabulary holds only approximately. Ataba, the threshold. Amanah, the trust held on behalf of others. Maʿrifa, knowledge by acquaintance rather than by proposition. Tarjama, translation, the movement of meaning between worlds. Each word is rendered in the script tradition that historically carried it: Kufi on thresholds and city walls, Naskh in contracts and correspondence, Nastaliq in courtly poetry, Ruqʿa in the everyday working hand. The reader encounters Arabic first, as substrate beneath the English text. The inversion is intentional.

The entries are cumulative rather than independent. Someone who stands at a threshold, holds their obligations as custodianship, and knows what they know through embodied experience will practice leadership as translation. The arc moves from position to ethic to epistemology to praxis.

The treatment of the letterform here is cultural and philosophical rather than devotional. It stands in the lineage of the Hurufiyya movement, the twentieth century Arab artists who took the letter itself as visual and intellectual material.

Entry Two · Ethic

أمانة Amanah · Custodianship

Accountability as a relation held, not a requirement met

Phase 2, Residency, June 15 to 19 · Kenny & Fraser (2012); Bodie et al. (2023)

Amanah appears here in Naskh, the balanced book hand that became the script of contracts, deeds, and correspondence because nothing in it hides from the reader. In everyday Arabic, amanah needs no institution: it is the parcel a neighbour leaves in your keeping, the duty you carry because someone trusted you with it. Cities name their municipal stewardship with the same word. This entry asks what accountability looks like when it is held as amanah rather than enforced as compliance.

Entry Three · Epistemology

معرفة Maʿrifa · Embodied Knowing

Knowledge arrived at through living, not through reading

Phase 3, Sessions 9 to 10 · Dedeurwaerdere (2024); Muia & Phillips (2023)

Maʿrifa hangs above this entry in Nastaliq, the cascading script of Persian courtly poetry, in which words descend rather than sit. Arabic distinguishes maʿrifa, the knowing of acquaintance, from ʿilm, the knowing of propositions; what French splits into connaître and savoir, Arabic carries in everyday speech. This entry is about the first kind: knowledge that accumulates through years of practice and through being met by the very systems one later administers, knowledge that pours rather than stacks.

Entry Four · Praxis

ترجمة Tarjama · Translation

Moving meaning between worlds that do not share a grammar

Phase 3, Sessions 11 to 12 · Travers (2022); Morse & Cook (2014)

Tarjama is rendered in Ruqʿa, the unceremonious working hand of Ottoman clerks, a script of notes and messages built for moving meaning quickly between people. The word holds an entire intellectual history: the Baghdad translation movement that carried Greek philosophy into Arabic and onward to Europe, the turjuman who stood between empires, and the classical sense of tarjama as a biography, a life rendered in writing. This entry is about translation as practice, the work the three earlier words make possible.
